Director of Critical Minerals Policy, US Department of Energy (or equivalent at the EU Commission, Chilean CORFO, or Indonesian Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources)

Who You Represent

State interests in balancing energy transition goals, economic competitiveness, national security, and regulatory obligations. This stakeholder's position differs by country — a US DOE official has different priorities than a Chilean CORFO official or an Indonesian ministry official — but in this scenario represents the US federal government's perspective on EV battery supply chain policy.

What's at Stake
  • The pace of domestic battery manufacturing capacity build-out relative to FEOC compliance deadlines
  • Mineral permitting timelines (US mine permitting often takes 7–10 years)
  • The impracticable-to-trace exemption for graphite expiring in 2026, exposing automakers to FEOC compliance obligations for a mineral that is 90%+ Chinese-processed
  • Lithium price volatility undermining investment in domestic projects
  • Political backlash against EV incentives under the Trump administration (2025) creating policy uncertainty
  • Balancing Indigenous land rights and environmental review against critical mineral project approvals.
Your Priorities & Evidence
  • Achieving US domestic battery and critical mineral supply chain self-sufficiency to reduce dependence on China
  • Implementing IRA incentive programs to create domestic manufacturing jobs
  • Enforcing FEOC restrictions to exclude adversary-nation supply chains
  • Supporting free trade agreements with lithium-producing countries (Chile, Australia, Japan, South Korea) to meet IRA critical mineral thresholds
  • Balancing environmental review requirements against the urgency of mining project permitting.
