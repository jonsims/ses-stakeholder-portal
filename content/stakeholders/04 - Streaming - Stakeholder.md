## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

---

**Stakeholder 1: Streaming Platform Infrastructure Executive**

- **Role/Title**: VP of Infrastructure / CTO at a major streaming company (e.g., Netflix, Spotify)
- **Who they represent**: Senior leadership responsible for the physical and cloud infrastructure that delivers streaming at scale; accountable to shareholders for service reliability, cost efficiency, and competitive positioning
- **Primary Interests**: Maintain low latency and high reliability; reduce infrastructure cost per stream; stay ahead of AI-driven infrastructure demand; secure long-term energy contracts; control supply chain for critical components
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Rising electricity costs as AI and data centers compete for grid capacity; undersea cable security and redundancy; rare earth supply chain disruptions; regulatory pressure on data center siting; growing public scrutiny of water and energy use
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 6 (music label executive — both benefit from platform growth); Stakeholder 7 (government regulator — seeks partnership on grid investment)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 3 (community resident — water and land conflicts); Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — wants slower growth and mandatory disclosures)

---

**Stakeholder 2: Independent Musician / Content Creator**

- **Role/Title**: Working musician with releases on Spotify and Apple Music; no major label affiliation
- **Who they represent**: The approximately tens of millions of independent artists whose livelihoods depend on streaming royalties; a demographic that has been economically restructured by the shift from physical media to streaming
- **Primary Interests**: Fair compensation per stream; transparent royalty accounting; protection from AI voice cloning and content displacement; algorithmic visibility for non-superstar artists
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Spotify's minimum 1,000-stream threshold effectively eliminates royalties for small artists; AI-generated music flooding the platform with content that cannibalizes stream counts and royalties; platforms' data opacity prevents artists from understanding how streaming economics work; the infrastructure investment being subsidized partly by low royalty rates
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 6 (music label executive — aligned on AI and royalty floor issues, though with different power dynamics); Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — both challenge platform power)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — structural adversary on royalty rates); Stakeholder 7 (regulator — tends to prioritize infrastructure investment over creator protections)

---

**Stakeholder 3: Data Center Host Community Resident**

- **Role/Title**: Resident and local advocacy organizer in a data center host community (e.g., Loudoun County, Virginia or County Meath, Ireland)
- **Who they represent**: Residents of communities where data center campuses have been built or are proposed; concerned citizens navigating the tradeoffs of infrastructure hosting — tax revenue and jobs vs. land use, water competition, electricity grid strain, and noise
- **Primary Interests**: Community quality of life; water and electricity access for residents and local agriculture; equitable sharing of infrastructure benefits; democratic input into land use decisions; transparency about what data centers actually consume
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Water withdrawal competition during drought conditions; electricity grid saturation pushing up prices for residential customers; data centers contributing little local employment while consuming enormous resources; tax incentive structures that benefit corporations but offer limited community return; noise and visual impact of large industrial facilities
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — provides data and credibility for advocacy); Stakeholder 7 (government regulator — the lever through which community interests are represented)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — has political influence and economic leverage over local governments); Stakeholder 6 (music label executive — indifferent to community infrastructure issues)

---

**Stakeholder 4: Environmental Researcher (Digital Carbon Footprint)**

- **Role/Title**: Senior researcher at a sustainability institute or university studying the lifecycle environmental impacts of digital infrastructure
- **Who they represent**: Scientific and environmental advocacy community; produces the data that informs policy and public debate; affiliated with institutions like the IEA, Carbon Trust, or academic sustainability departments
- **Primary Interests**: Accurate measurement and disclosure of streaming's environmental footprint; life cycle methods that capture embodied carbon in hardware, not just operational energy; policy frameworks that internalize environmental externalities; data center siting standards that protect water and land
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Misleading "per stream" carbon metrics that obscure total system impact; lack of mandatory disclosure by platforms; server refresh cycles generating e-waste; rare earth mining impacts excluded from corporate sustainability reporting; AI acceleration of infrastructure buildout outpacing efficiency gains
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 3 (community resident — shared interest in transparency and accountability); Stakeholder 2 (independent musician — both challenge platform power from outside it)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — prefers voluntary, favorable metrics); Stakeholder 7 (regulator — often captured by industry interests or focused on economic development over environmental goals)

---

**Stakeholder 5: Undersea Cable Engineer / Submarine Cable Operator**

- **Role/Title**: Senior engineer or project director at a submarine cable company (e.g., SubCom, Alcatel Submarine Networks) or at a telecommunications carrier's undersea cable division
- **Who they represent**: The specialized industrial sector responsible for manufacturing, laying, and maintaining undersea fiber optic cables; a relatively small industry (approximately 60 cable ships globally) with disproportionate importance to global internet infrastructure
- **Primary Interests**: Contract revenue from hyperscaler cable build-outs; technical standards and route safety; geopolitical navigation (Red Sea disruptions, China-US tensions over cable routes); environmental permitting for shore landings; long-term infrastructure investment stability
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Big Tech's move to own and operate their own cables is disintermediating traditional telecom carriers; geopolitical tensions are restricting optimal route choices (US-China tensions have effectively blocked some Pacific routes); security vulnerabilities of undersea infrastructure are increasing; environmental permitting for cable landings is becoming more complex; the shift to AI-driven demand is accelerating build-out pressure beyond current industry capacity
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — the primary client relationship); Stakeholder 7 (government regulator — governments share interest in infrastructure security)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — cable landings face environmental scrutiny); Stakeholder 3 (community resident — shore landing sites sometimes face community opposition)

---

**Stakeholder 6: Major Music Label Executive**

- **Role/Title**: SVP of Digital Strategy at a major label (Universal Music Group, Sony Music Entertainment, or Warner Music Group)
- **Who they represent**: The three major music labels that collectively control approximately 65-70% of recorded music rights globally and act as powerful intermediaries between artists and streaming platforms
- **Primary Interests**: Maximize per-stream royalty rates; maintain leverage over streaming platforms through licensing negotiations; protect catalog value against AI-generated music; ensure the label's share of royalty pools is maximized relative to independent distributors and publishers
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: AI-generated music threatens to dilute streaming royalty pools; Spotify's royalty model changes (minimum stream threshold) disproportionately benefit the long tail at major labels' expense; infrastructure investments by platforms make them more powerful negotiating partners; platform consolidation reduces competitive pressure; transparency into streaming data remains a structural power imbalance favoring platforms
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 2 (independent musician — on AI and royalty floor issues, though labels and independents have historically antagonistic relationships); Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — co-dependent relationship; labels need platforms and vice versa)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — different priorities entirely); Stakeholder 5 (undersea cable engineer — no direct conflict, but indifferent to content-side economics)

---

**Stakeholder 7: Government Energy and Utility Regulator**

- **Role/Title**: Director of a state or national energy regulatory body, or elected official overseeing data center policy (e.g., Virginia General Assembly member, Ireland's Commission for the Regulation of Utilities)
- **Who they represent**: The public interest as mediated through government — balancing economic development goals (job creation, tax revenue), grid reliability, environmental obligations, and community welfare
- **Primary Interests**: Grid stability and reliability; meeting renewable energy and emissions targets; maximizing economic development from data center investment; protecting consumers from electricity price increases driven by data center demand; navigating the political economy of tax incentive programs
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage)**: Data center electricity demand growing faster than renewable capacity can be added; tax incentive programs costing states billions in foregone revenue (Virginia: $1 billion in FY2024); lack of transparency about data center water and energy consumption makes regulation difficult; the AI buildout is accelerating demand in ways that may compromise grid decarbonization timelines; international competition for data center investment creates a race-to-the-bottom on regulatory standards
- **Likely Allies**: Stakeholder 3 (community resident — regulator represents community interests through policy); Stakeholder 5 (undersea cable engineer — shared interest in infrastructure security)
- **Likely Opponents**: Stakeholder 1 (platform executive — seeks maximum regulatory flexibility); Stakeholder 4 (environmental researcher — often advocates for faster and stricter regulation than political economy allows)
