## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

The following eight stakeholders represent the primary actors with materially distinct interests, concerns, and positions in the extraction and production stage of the TV lifecycle. They have been selected to ensure genuine tensions and maximum coverage of the supply chain.

---

**Stakeholder 1**
- **Role/Title:** Senior Vice President, Supply Chain and Sustainability — Major TV Brand (e.g., Samsung Electronics or LG Electronics)
- **Who they represent:** Vertically integrated consumer electronics corporations that design, market, and (in part) manufacture televisions; responsible to shareholders, boards, and increasingly to ESG-focused institutional investors.
- **Primary Interests:** Maintaining cost-competitive material supply chains; protecting brand reputation from adverse sustainability headlines; meeting voluntary ESG commitments and mandatory supply chain disclosure requirements (e.g., EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive); securing preferential access to critical materials (indium, rare earths) as geopolitical supply risk rises.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Disruption to rare earth or indium supply from Chinese export controls; reputational damage from conflict mineral exposure in the DRC supply chain; rising panel procurement costs; competitive disadvantage relative to Chinese brands with cheaper, state-subsidized panel supply; regulatory compliance costs for supply chain due diligence.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 6 (Trade Association), Stakeholder 8 (Recycling Advocate — on circular supply security)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 4 (Mining Community Resident), Stakeholder 3 (Fab Worker), Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO)

---

**Stakeholder 2**
- **Role/Title:** Director of Operations — Chinese Rare Earth Mining and Processing Company (e.g., China Northern Rare Earth Group or state-affiliated Jiangxi miner)
- **Who they represent:** State-owned and state-affiliated enterprises that dominate global rare earth extraction and processing in China; answerable to provincial and national government industrial policy as well as commercial objectives.
- **Primary Interests:** Maximizing export value of rare earth compounds; supporting Chinese government strategic goals of controlling critical material supply chains; managing environmental remediation costs and regulatory compliance domestically; resisting international pressure to accelerate environmental cleanup before economic returns are secured.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** International efforts to develop alternative rare earth supply chains (threatening China's market dominance); domestic environmental regulations that increase operating costs; international scrutiny of environmental conditions at mine sites; US/EU efforts to recycle rare earths from e-waste (reducing primary material demand).
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 7 (Government Trade Policy Official — Chinese interests)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO), Stakeholder 4 (Mining Community Resident), Stakeholder 8 (Recycling Advocate)

---

**Stakeholder 3**
- **Role/Title:** Display Panel Factory Worker / Occupational Health Advocate (South Korean LCD/OLED Fab)
- **Who they represent:** Current and former production workers in South Korean and Chinese display panel manufacturing facilities; connected to labor advocacy organizations such as SHARPS (Korea) and GoodElectronics; representing thousands of workers who experienced or fear occupational illness.
- **Primary Interests:** Legally enforceable rights to comprehensive chemical exposure disclosure; meaningful compensation for work-related illness without requiring individual workers to prove causation against corporate opacity; independent health monitoring programs; strengthened South Korean occupational safety law; international standards for hazardous chemical disclosure in semiconductor and display manufacturing.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Continued corporate resistance to full transparency on chemical processes used in fab environments; inadequate application of occupational standards in newer Chinese display fabs that lack the labor law infrastructure of South Korea; lack of international enforcement mechanisms for workplace chemical safety in supply chains.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO), Stakeholder 4 (Mining Community Resident)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive), Stakeholder 2 (Mining Company), Stakeholder 6 (Trade Association)

---

**Stakeholder 4**
- **Role/Title:** Community Representative — Rare Earth Mining Village, Jiangxi Province, China
- **Who they represent:** Residents of communities surrounding ion-adsorption rare earth mining operations in southern China — including farmers, fishing families, and village health workers — who experience the environmental externalities of mining without corresponding economic benefits; represents displaced and at-risk populations.
- **Primary Interests:** Clean water and soil remediation funded by mining companies and the government; fair compensation for agricultural land rendered unproductive by mine drainage; meaningful participation in decisions about continued or expanded mining operations; transparent health monitoring and medical support for impacted community members.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Ongoing leaching of heavy metals and nitrogen compounds into drinking water sources; government prioritization of rare earth revenues over community health; inadequate enforcement of environmental remediation commitments by mining companies; the 38-billion-yuan cleanup debt that remains largely unfunded; erosion and hillside instability threatening homes and farmland.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO), Stakeholder 3 (Fab Worker)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 2 (Mining Company), Stakeholder 7 (Government Trade Policy Official), Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive — indirectly sustains demand)

---

**Stakeholder 5**
- **Role/Title:** Senior Campaigner — Environmental NGO (e.g., Global Witness, Earthworks, or China-focused environmental law organization)
- **Who they represent:** International and domestic civil society organizations focused on the environmental and human rights dimensions of extractive industries feeding into consumer electronics supply chains; their constituency includes affected communities, donors, and the broader public.
- **Primary Interests:** Mandatory supply chain due diligence for mining-linked environmental harms (beyond existing conflict mineral frameworks that focus on human rights narrowly); stronger international standards for rare earth mine closure and remediation; accelerated development of indium and rare earth recycling infrastructure; restrictions on expansion of mining in ecologically sensitive areas; corporate liability for supply chain environmental damage.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Rare earth mine tailings and acid drainage contamination; near-zero recycling rates for critical materials leading to resource depletion and expanded mining pressure; conflict mineral financing through coltan in DRC; lack of transparency and independent monitoring at Chinese and Myanmar mine sites; inadequate corporate response to community health impacts.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 3 (Fab Worker), Stakeholder 4 (Mining Community Resident), Stakeholder 8 (Recycling Advocate)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 2 (Mining Company), Stakeholder 6 (Trade Association), Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive — on pace of reform)

---

**Stakeholder 6**
- **Role/Title:** Vice President of Government Affairs — Consumer Technology Association (CTA) or equivalent industry trade body
- **Who they represent:** The collective interests of the consumer electronics industry in the US and internationally, including TV brands, component manufacturers, and retailers; CTA is North America's largest technology trade association, representing companies that support over 18 million US jobs.
- **Primary Interests:** Maintaining market access for global supply chains; advocating for voluntary rather than mandatory sustainability standards; opposing tariffs that raise component costs; promoting industry-led recycling initiatives over mandatory extended producer responsibility schemes; representing the industry's position that efficiency improvements (energy per screen area down 76% since 2003) demonstrate responsible self-governance.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Mandatory supply chain due diligence legislation that imposes compliance costs and legal liability; tariffs on Chinese panels and components that raise TV prices for US consumers; proposed regulations on hazardous chemical disclosure; any regulatory move toward mandatory minimum product lifespan or software support periods that would constrain product design decisions.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive), Stakeholder 7 (Government Trade Policy Official — on tariff reduction)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO), Stakeholder 3 (Fab Worker), Stakeholder 8 (Recycling Advocate — on extended producer responsibility)

---

**Stakeholder 7**
- **Role/Title:** Deputy Assistant Secretary — Office of Critical Minerals and Supply Chain Resilience, US Department of Commerce (or equivalent EU/national government official)
- **Who they represent:** National government interests in securing supply chains for critical materials essential to both consumer electronics and national security/defense applications; responsible for translating geopolitical risk into policy instruments.
- **Primary Interests:** Reducing dependence on Chinese rare earth supply and processing; building domestic or allied-nation alternatives (e.g., Lynas in Australia, Mountain Pass in California, Rare Earth processing partnerships with Canada); using trade policy (tariffs, export controls, CHIPS Act-adjacent programs) to reshore critical materials production; ensuring rare earth supply security is addressed in bilateral trade negotiations.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** China's demonstrated willingness to use rare earth export controls as geopolitical leverage (2025 episode); the inadequacy of current recycling infrastructure as a domestic supply offset; the long timelines required to permit and build alternative mines and processing facilities; the risk that purely market-based approaches will not deliver supply chain security at the required pace.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive — on supply security, not on tariff costs), Stakeholder 8 (Recycling Advocate — on domestic material recovery)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 2 (Mining Company — Chinese interests), Stakeholder 6 (Trade Association — on tariffs)

---

**Stakeholder 8**
- **Role/Title:** Director of Policy — Electronics Recycling and Right-to-Repair Advocacy Organization (e.g., Electronics TakeBack Coalition, iFixit Policy Team)
- **Who they represent:** A coalition of interests including e-waste recycling processors, right-to-repair advocates, environmental justice organizations, and material recovery researchers; focused on the end-of-life stage but with a systemic view that connects material choices at the design/production stage to recyclability outcomes.
- **Primary Interests:** Mandatory Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) legislation that makes TV brands financially responsible for end-of-life collection and recycling; design standards requiring TVs to be built for disassembly and material recovery; minimum software support periods (currently being advanced in several US states and in the EU) to extend TV usable life and reduce replacement frequency; development of economically viable indium and rare earth recovery from LCD panels; opposition to design practices (adhesive bonding, proprietary fasteners) that prevent repair.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Design decisions made during production that effectively make downstream recycling of critical materials (indium, rare earths) impossible at scale — particularly the fusion bonding of LCD glass stacks; the near-zero indium recycling rate despite indium's classification as critical and irreplaceable; planned obsolescence via software cutoffs that drives premature TV replacement and increased mining demand; the gap between the 11 US states with EPR legislation and the rest of the country.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 5 (Environmental NGO), Stakeholder 7 (Government Official — on supply security through domestic material recovery), Stakeholder 3 (Fab Worker — shared interest in corporate accountability)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 6 (Trade Association), Stakeholder 1 (TV Brand Executive — on design mandates and EPR costs)
