## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

The following seven stakeholders represent the most productive set of perspectives for classroom discussion: they are real, their interests are legitimately in tension, and they collectively span the environmental, social, economic, labor, and regulatory dimensions of the extraction and production stage.

---

**Stakeholder 1: Industrial Beef Packer / Integrator Executive**

- **Role/Title:** Vice President of Operations, Major Meatpacking Corporation (e.g., JBS USA, Tyson, Cargill)
- **Who they represent:** The senior management and shareholders of the consolidated meatpacking industry; the Big Four companies that collectively process 85% of US beef
- **Primary Interests:** Maximizing throughput and margin in commodity processing; defending market position against antitrust scrutiny and alternative proteins; managing supply chain costs; maintaining relationships with retail and food service customers; protecting shareholder value
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Feed price volatility and cattle supply constraints; labor cost and turnover in processing plants; regulatory compliance costs (FSIS, EPA); reputational risk from deforestation linkages or price-fixing suits; potential for further antitrust enforcement to force divestitures or structural changes; maintaining line speeds against OSHA pressure
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 2 (contract farmer — needs the integrator relationship to survive), Stakeholder 7 (food safety regulator — shares interest in stable, large-scale inspection infrastructure)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 3 (meatpacking worker — directly in conflict over wages and line speeds), Stakeholder 4 (environmental NGO — deforestation exposure), Stakeholder 5 (animal welfare advocate — confinement practices)

---

**Stakeholder 2: Contract Poultry/Hog Grower**

- **Role/Title:** Contract Livestock Grower, Mid-Scale Family Farm (e.g., poultry grower in Arkansas or hog farmer in Iowa)
- **Who they represent:** The estimated tens of thousands of US farmers who raise chickens or hogs under production contracts with integrators; farmers who own infrastructure and labor but not the animals
- **Primary Interests:** Securing and maintaining a viable contract; servicing barn construction debt; avoiding pay docks and tournament-system losses; seeking regulatory protection from integrator abuses; preserving the family farm as an economic unit even within a constrained system
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Being supplied with substandard chicks or hogs; late or incorrect feed deliveries that result in pay docks; contract termination risk with millions in debt remaining; inability to negotiate contract terms; proposed regulatory rollbacks that reduce already-thin protections; integrator consolidation narrowing available processors
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (integrator — their survival depends on this relationship, though conflicted), Stakeholder 7 (food safety regulator — regulatory structure provides some accountability framework)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (integrator — in direct economic conflict over contract terms and tournament pay), Stakeholder 4 (environmental NGO — CAFOs they operate generate odor and water quality concerns)

---

**Stakeholder 3: Meatpacking Plant Worker / Labor Organizer**

- **Role/Title:** Meatpacking Line Worker and/or Union Representative (e.g., United Food and Commercial Workers International Union — UFCW)
- **Who they represent:** The approximately 500,000 US meatpacking workers; disproportionately Latinx (34.9%), immigrant (42% foreign-born), and undocumented (estimated 30–50%); among the most economically vulnerable and physically endangered workers in the US economy
- **Primary Interests:** Workplace safety (reduced line speeds, protective equipment, ergonomic reforms); legal immigration status protections that reduce exploitability; living wages and the right to organize; workers' compensation access; protection from retaliation; an end to the structural use of immigration enforcement as a union-busting tool
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Injury rates three times the national average; 27 workers per day hospitalized or amputated; 50% real wage decline since 1975; COVID-19 vulnerabilities from forced continued operations; risk of deportation reducing willingness to report violations; production line speed increases driven by productivity pressure; limited OSHA enforcement in processing plants
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 5 (animal welfare advocate — shares interest in slowing line speeds and increasing transparency), Stakeholder 6 (rural health advocate — shares concern about community impacts of large facilities)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (meatpacking executive — direct conflict over wages and line speeds), Stakeholder 7 (food safety regulator — limited enforcement of worker protections has enabled status quo)

---

**Stakeholder 4: Environmental NGO — Deforestation and Climate Focus**

- **Role/Title:** Senior Campaigner, International Environmental Organization (e.g., Greenpeace, Mighty Earth, Rainforest Action Network)
- **Who they represent:** Global climate and biodiversity interests; Indigenous communities in Amazon and Cerrado whose territories are being encroached upon; the scientific community's warnings about tipping points in tropical ecosystems
- **Primary Interests:** Ending deforestation linked to beef and feed crop supply chains; achieving binding, traceable zero-deforestation commitments from major buyers; redirecting capital toward protein transition; holding corporations legally and reputationally accountable for supply chain harms; strengthening EU Deforestation Regulation and equivalent US policies
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** JBS's demonstrated failure to meet its 2025 deforestation pledge; cattle laundering through intermediary farms that makes supply chain tracing unreliable; expansion of soy in the unprotected Cerrado; JBS's reported linkage to cattle raised on Indigenous territories; state-level ag-gag laws blocking investigative access; antibiotic resistance from CAFOs as a cross-cutting public health threat
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 5 (animal welfare advocate — shares interest in ending industrial confinement systems), Stakeholder 6 (rural community health advocate — shares concern about CAFO externalities)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (meatpacking executive — directly threatened by deforestation campaigns), Stakeholder 2 (contract farmer — deforestation campaigns can reduce market access for conventional livestock products)

---

**Stakeholder 5: Animal Welfare Advocate**

- **Role/Title:** Farm Animal Policy Director, Animal Protection Organization (e.g., Humane Society of the United States, Animal Welfare Institute, ASPCA)
- **Who they represent:** Approximately 10 billion land animals raised for food in the US each year; consumers who have preferences about how their food is produced; the 46% of US egg-laying hens now in cage-free systems
- **Primary Interests:** Ending extreme confinement in battery cages, gestation crates, and veal crates; slowing production line speeds that cause inhumane handling; reducing antibiotic use driven by overcrowded conditions; supporting ag-gag law challenges to maintain investigative access; expanding cage-free, pasture-raised, and higher-welfare certification systems; pressing for a federal animal welfare law covering farmed animals (the Animal Welfare Act currently exempts farm animals)
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Industrial confinement systems remain the dominant production model despite years of corporate pledges; ag-gag laws (eight states with active laws) block documentation of abuse; antibiotic overuse directly reflects overcrowded, high-stress conditions; Arizona delayed its cage-free egg law to 2034 citing the 2024–2025 H5N1 bird flu outbreak; line speed increases accelerate inhumane handling at slaughter; Federal Farm Bill has not included meaningful animal welfare provisions
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental NGO — share opposition to industrial CAFO model), Stakeholder 3 (meatpacking worker — share interest in slowing line speeds and increasing facility transparency)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (meatpacking executive — welfare standards increase costs and reduce throughput), Stakeholder 2 (contract grower — welfare mandates may require costly barn retrofits)

---

**Stakeholder 6: Rural Community Health Advocate**

- **Role/Title:** Community Health Researcher / Environmental Justice Advocate (e.g., North Carolina Environmental Justice Network, Iowa Citizens for Community Improvement)
- **Who they represent:** Rural residents — disproportionately Black, Indigenous, and low-income — living in close proximity to large CAFOs in states like North Carolina and Iowa; communities experiencing documented health disparities linked to CAFO siting; rural well users exposed to nitrate contamination
- **Primary Interests:** Stricter siting regulations and setback distances for new CAFOs from homes, schools, and churches; mandatory environmental impact assessments before new facility permits; enforceable limits on lagoon application near waterways and private wells; funding for community health monitoring; legal accountability for corporations whose operations cause documented health damage; recognition of CAFO siting as an environmental justice issue
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** North Carolina research documenting elevated mortality, kidney disease, and anemia near hog CAFOs; higher asthma rates in children near Iowa hog barns; nitrate contamination of rural wells from manure application; H₂S and ammonia emissions causing headaches, respiratory symptoms, and reduced quality of life; disproportionate siting of CAFOs in communities of color; limited CAFO regulation at the state level; rollback of EPA rules governing CAFO reporting
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental NGO — shares concern about CAFO pollution), Stakeholder 3 (meatpacking worker — shares concern about corporate accountability and community impacts)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (meatpacking executive — opposes siting restrictions and enhanced regulation), Stakeholder 2 (contract grower — new CAFO regulations increase compliance costs and may threaten viability of existing operations)

---

**Stakeholder 7: Government Food Safety Regulator**

- **Role/Title:** District Circuit Supervisor or Policy Official, USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service (FSIS)
- **Who they represent:** The federal mandate to ensure the safety of the US meat, poultry, and egg supply; the approximately 9,000 FSIS inspectors required by law to be present in every federally inspected slaughter facility every day it operates; public health consumers nationwide
- **Primary Interests:** Maintaining sufficient staffing to fulfill the legal inspection mandate at 6,800 meat, poultry, and egg product plants; updating food safety standards to address emerging pathogens (Listeria, Salmonella); navigating the regulatory boundary with FDA for cultivated meat; balancing industry-requested line speed increases against public health evidence; operating within a constrained and shrinking federal budget
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Workforce attrition and difficulty recruiting Public Health Veterinarians (mission-critical field positions); DOGE-era federal workforce reductions threatening inspection capacity; a 2024 multistate Listeria outbreak that killed 10 people linked to ready-to-eat meat products; growing evidence that faster production line speeds increase contamination risk; ambiguity in regulating novel proteins including cultivated meat and fermentation-derived products; state-level fragmentation (seven states banning cultivated meat) complicating national regulatory coherence; balancing transparency obligations with industry-sensitive data
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (meatpacking executive — shares interest in a stable, predictable regulatory framework), Stakeholder 2 (contract farmer — regulatory structure provides some market order)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 3 (meatpacking worker — workers argue FSIS does not adequately enforce worker safety or line speed limits), Stakeholder 5 (animal welfare advocate — FSIS enforcement of humane handling at slaughter is viewed as insufficient)
