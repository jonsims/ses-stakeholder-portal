## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

---

**Stakeholder 1 — EV Automaker Executive**

- **Role/Title:** Vice President of Battery Sourcing & Supply Chain, Major US Automaker (e.g., GM, Ford, or Stellantis)
- **Who they represent:** The strategic and financial interests of a large publicly traded US automaker attempting to transition its product portfolio from ICE to BEV vehicles while remaining competitive and profitable.
- **Primary Interests:** Achieving cost parity with ICE vehicles; qualifying for IRA tax credits to make EVs price-competitive for consumers; securing a stable, long-term supply of critical minerals at predictable prices; protecting market share against Chinese EV manufacturers; managing investor expectations around EV division losses.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** The high cost and carbon intensity of battery manufacturing; FEOC restrictions making it difficult to use lower-cost Chinese-processed materials; lithium and cobalt price volatility undermining production cost forecasts; the time and capital required to build domestic supply chain capacity; reputational risk from human rights abuses in upstream supply chains (cobalt/DRC).
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholders 4 (battery materials company) and 7 (government regulator, if IRA supportive)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholders 5 (autoworker union, on labor terms) and 3 (Indigenous community leader, on mineral sourcing practices)

---

**Stakeholder 2 — Artisanal Cobalt Miner (DRC)**

- **Role/Title:** Artisanal and Small-Scale Miner (Creuseur), Lualaba Province, Democratic Republic of Congo
- **Who they represent:** One of approximately 255,000 artisanal cobalt miners in the DRC — adults and children — who dig by hand in informal mines (known locally as "creuseurs"), often on the periphery of industrial concessions, selling raw ore to intermediaries for less than $2/day.
- **Primary Interests:** Economic survival and income for their family in a context of extreme poverty and limited formal employment alternatives; physical safety in the absence of mine safety equipment or regulation; access to a fair share of the value generated by cobalt their labor extracts.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Risk of tunnel collapse, respiratory disease from cobalt dust, and chemical exposure; child labor — including their own children — because household income depends on all family members working; debt bondage to ore-buying intermediaries; the 2024 court ruling absolving tech and automotive companies of liability for conditions in the supply chain; lack of a formal mechanism for their communities to benefit from the global EV transition.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 6 (environmental justice advocate)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholders 1 (automaker executive, whose sourcing practices perpetuate the system) and 4 (battery materials company, whose profit margins depend on low-cost cobalt)

---

**Stakeholder 3 — Indigenous Community Leader (Atacama, Chile)**

- **Role/Title:** Council Member, Atacameño Peoples (Lickanantay), Salar de Atacama, Chile
- **Who they represent:** Indigenous Atacameño communities whose ancestral territory, cultural practices, and subsistence livelihoods are directly threatened by lithium brine extraction in the Atacama salt flat — one of the driest inhabited environments on Earth.
- **Primary Interests:** Protection of water rights and aquifer systems that sustain both human communities and the fragile Atacama ecosystem (flamingo breeding grounds, wetlands, salt flat biodiversity); genuine free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) over any mining activity on ancestral territory; a just distribution of revenues from lithium extracted from their lands; the right to say no to extraction.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Documented aquifer depletion caused by brine extraction — 65% of Atacama water consumption attributable to lithium mines; land subsidence around extraction wells (documented in 2024 research); SQM's court-found violation of brine withdrawal limits; the 2018 community agreement with SQM that many leaders regard as illegitimate and non-consensual; Chile's national lithium strategy potentially overriding community opposition; the irony of their land being destroyed to manufacture "green" technology for wealthy consumers in other countries.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 6 (environmental justice advocate)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholders 1 (automaker executive) and 7 (government mining regulator, if prioritizing production targets over community rights)

---

**Stakeholder 4 — Battery Materials Company Executive**

- **Role/Title:** Chief Commercial Officer, Cathode Active Materials Producer (e.g., Umicore, Livent/Allkem, or a Chinese cathode producer such as Huayou Cobalt or GEM Co.)
- **Who they represent:** The interests of a company that occupies the midstream of the EV battery supply chain — processing raw minerals (lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese) into battery-grade cathode active materials (CAM) and supplying them to battery cell manufacturers.
- **Primary Interests:** Locking in long-term supply agreements with automakers; maintaining processing margins amid mineral price volatility; expanding capacity to meet projected demand growth; navigating IRA FEOC restrictions (which may classify certain Chinese-affiliated processors as restricted suppliers); developing supply chain traceability systems that satisfy EU battery passport requirements.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Lithium price collapse (2023–2024) undermining the economics of upstream mining investments; cobalt supply vulnerability given DRC concentration; FEOC compliance costs for companies with Chinese ownership or partnerships; capital intensity of building new processing capacity in non-FEOC jurisdictions; battery chemistry shifts toward LFP (which bypasses traditional NMC supply chains) threatening existing processing investments.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (automaker executive) and Stakeholder 7 (government regulator, through IRA support for domestic processing)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholders 2 (DRC miner, whose labor conditions are the industry's central human rights liability) and 3 (Indigenous community leader)

---

**Stakeholder 5 — Autoworker Union Representative**

- **Role/Title:** Regional Director, United Auto Workers (UAW), or Shop Steward, IG Metall (Germany)
- **Who they represent:** The interests of workers employed in vehicle assembly and battery manufacturing — both established ICE-era workers facing an uncertain transition, and newer workers at EV and battery plants where union representation is being actively contested.
- **Primary Interests:** Job security during the EV transition; fair wages and benefits at new battery gigafactories; union recognition and collective bargaining rights at Tesla and other non-union EV manufacturers; ensuring that the green transition does not come at the cost of worker rights and pay; safety standards in battery manufacturing environments (chemical exposures, repetitive strain injuries).
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** Tesla's sustained resistance to union organizing (NLRB complaints, alleged retaliatory firings); the use of temporary or foreign contract workers at battery plants in lieu of permanent unionized positions; the high capital intensity of battery manufacturing potentially replacing labor with automation; GM and Ford EV division losses leading to production pauses and layoffs; whether IRA domestic manufacturing incentives translate into good union jobs or simply subsidize non-union facilities.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 6 (environmental justice advocate, on labor rights dimension) and partially Stakeholder 7 (government regulator, through IRA's domestic job creation goals)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (automaker executive, on wages and unionization) and partially Stakeholder 4 (battery materials company, which may prefer flexible/contract labor)

---

**Stakeholder 6 — Environmental Justice Advocate**

- **Role/Title:** Senior Researcher or Campaign Director, Environmental and Human Rights NGO (e.g., Earthworks, Global Witness, or Amnesty International's Business & Human Rights program)
- **Who they represent:** A coalition of affected communities, civil society organizations, and researchers who document and advocate against the environmental and human rights harms embedded in the EV battery supply chain — with particular focus on frontline communities in the DRC, Atacama, and Indonesian nickel-mining regions.
- **Primary Interests:** Binding corporate accountability for supply chain harms; mandatory human rights and environmental due diligence legislation; FPIC requirements for mining on Indigenous lands; child labor elimination in artisanal cobalt mining; pollution remediation in nickel-processing communities; ensuring the transition to EVs does not replicate the exploitation patterns of fossil fuel extraction in a new geography.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** The 2024 court ruling dismissing the DRC cobalt child labor lawsuit, signaling inadequate legal accountability for supply chain harms; Indonesia's coal-powered nickel smelting emitting SO2 and generating toxic tailings with minimal regulatory enforcement; greenwashing by automakers who market EVs as "clean" while externalities are borne by communities in the Global South; battery passport and traceability regimes that are voluntary or lack independent verification; lithium mining water depletion threatening Atacama communities; corporate capture of government regulators.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholders 2 (DRC miner) and 3 (Indigenous community leader)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholders 1 (automaker executive), 4 (battery materials company), and potentially 7 (government regulator, if regulatory capture is a concern)

---

**Stakeholder 7 — Government Mining and Trade Regulator**

- **Role/Title:** Director of Critical Minerals Policy, US Department of Energy (or equivalent at the EU Commission, Chilean CORFO, or Indonesian Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources)
- **Who they represent:** State interests in balancing energy transition goals, economic competitiveness, national security, and regulatory obligations. This stakeholder's position differs by country — a US DOE official has different priorities than a Chilean CORFO official or an Indonesian ministry official — but in this scenario represents the US federal government's perspective on EV battery supply chain policy.
- **Primary Interests:** Achieving US domestic battery and critical mineral supply chain self-sufficiency to reduce dependence on China; implementing IRA incentive programs to create domestic manufacturing jobs; enforcing FEOC restrictions to exclude adversary-nation supply chains; supporting free trade agreements with lithium-producing countries (Chile, Australia, Japan, South Korea) to meet IRA critical mineral thresholds; balancing environmental review requirements against the urgency of mining project permitting.
- **Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):** The pace of domestic battery manufacturing capacity build-out relative to FEOC compliance deadlines; mineral permitting timelines (US mine permitting often takes 7–10 years); the impracticable-to-trace exemption for graphite expiring in 2026, exposing automakers to FEOC compliance obligations for a mineral that is 90%+ Chinese-processed; lithium price volatility undermining investment in domestic projects; political backlash against EV incentives under the Trump administration (2025) creating policy uncertainty; balancing Indigenous land rights and environmental review against critical mineral project approvals.
- **Likely Allies:** Stakeholders 1 (automaker executive) and 4 (battery materials company) on supply chain buildout; partially Stakeholder 5 (union, on domestic job creation)
- **Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 3 (Indigenous community leader, on permitting speed vs. consultation requirements) and Stakeholder 6 (environmental justice advocate, on regulatory stringency)
