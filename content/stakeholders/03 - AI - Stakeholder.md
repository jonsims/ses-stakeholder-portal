## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

### Stakeholder 1: AI Technology Company Executive

**Role/Title:** Senior Vice President of Infrastructure, Major AI Lab (OpenAI / Google DeepMind / Meta AI)

**Who they represent:** The leadership of frontier AI development companies responsible for training and deploying large language models. These individuals oversee decisions about data center siting, energy sourcing, hardware procurement, training dataset composition, and the pace of model releases.

**Primary Interests:**
- Maintaining competitive position by training more powerful models faster
- Securing sufficient GPU compute (Nvidia H100/B200 clusters) and stable energy supply at scale
- Managing reputational risk from environmental and labor controversies
- Generating revenue from AI products to justify massive infrastructure investment

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- GPU supply bottlenecks from TSMC capacity constraints and export control uncertainty
- Rising energy costs and grid instability threatening data center uptime
- Regulatory pressure on training data sourcing following copyright lawsuits
- Reputational damage from cobalt supply chain and content moderator labor conditions

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 2 (Nvidia supply chain), Stakeholder 7 (government regulator supportive of U.S. AI competitiveness)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice advocate), Stakeholder 5 (artist/writer), Stakeholder 3 (DRC artisanal miner)

---

### Stakeholder 2: Nvidia / TSMC Supply Chain Manager

**Role/Title:** Director of Advanced Packaging Operations, TSMC (or equivalent Nvidia supply chain lead)

**Who they represent:** The semiconductor manufacturing ecosystem — specifically TSMC's fab workers and operations in Taiwan, Nvidia's supply chain organization, and the broader ecosystem of substrate suppliers, memory manufacturers (SK Hynix, Micron), and CoWoS packaging partners. This stakeholder sits at the chokepoint of the entire AI production chain.

**Primary Interests:**
- Expanding CoWoS and advanced packaging capacity to meet AI chip demand without compromising yield
- Maintaining TSMC's technological lead over Samsung and Intel fabs
- Managing geopolitical risk from Taiwan Strait tensions and U.S. government pressure to diversify fabrication to Arizona and Japan
- Securing reliable supplies of ultrapure water, specialty gases, and rare process chemicals

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Water scarcity risk in Taiwan affecting fab operations
- Worker chemical exposure in clean rooms and long-term health liability
- Disruption to rare earth and specialty chemical supply chains if China imposes export restrictions on gallium or germanium
- U.S. CHIPS Act compliance requirements tied to export controls

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company executive), Stakeholder 7 (government regulator — CHIPS Act support)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice advocate — water use), Stakeholder 3 (DRC miner — supply chain pressure)

---

### Stakeholder 3: DRC Artisanal Cobalt and Coltan Miner

**Role/Title:** Artisanal and Small-Scale Miner (ASM), Katanga Province or North Kivu, DRC

**Who they represent:** The estimated 150,000–200,000 artisanal miners in the DRC who extract cobalt and coltan using hand tools in informal mine sites, often under dangerous conditions. Many are economically coerced; an estimated 40,000 children work in or around mining sites. This stakeholder represents the most economically vulnerable node in the entire AI hardware supply chain and has virtually no negotiating power with downstream buyers.

**Primary Interests:**
- Obtaining a living wage for mining labor (current wages range from $2–10/day)
- Access to safety equipment, medical care, and protection from armed groups controlling mine access
- Formal recognition and legal protection for artisanal mining as a livelihood activity
- Removal of exploitative middlemen and direct access to certified purchasing programs

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Physical safety: tunnel collapses, respiratory disease from mineral dust, chemical exposure
- Child labor: economic conditions force family members including children to participate
- Conflict minerals: armed militia groups in eastern DRC tax or control coltan mining sites
- Erasure: supply chain auditing systems often exclude artisanal miners rather than improving their conditions

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice advocate — shared concern about extractive industry harms), Stakeholder 6 (AI safety researcher — some overlap on ethics of AI development costs)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company — benefits from low mineral costs), Stakeholder 2 (Nvidia/TSMC — supply chain cost pressure)

---

### Stakeholder 4: Environmental Justice Advocate — Data Center Host Community

**Role/Title:** Director of Environmental Justice, Community Coalition (e.g., Piedmont Environmental Council, Virginia; or equivalent in Iowa, Ireland, Singapore)

**Who they represent:** Residents and environmental organizations in communities hosting or targeted for large-scale data center construction. In Virginia's Loudoun and Prince William Counties — home to the world's densest data center cluster — this includes residents facing noise, visual impact, electricity rate increases, water competition, and exclusion from land use decisions. Nationally, more than 200 environmental organizations have aligned around calls for a moratorium.

**Primary Interests:**
- Meaningful community voice in data center siting and permitting decisions
- Transparent disclosure of water usage, energy consumption, and grid impact
- Equitable distribution of tax revenues generated by data centers to host communities
- Preservation of agricultural land, historic sites, and viewsheds from industrial development

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Electricity rate increases: grid transmission upgrades are socialized to all ratepayers
- Water withdrawals from stressed regional aquifers and rivers
- Loss of political power as data center revenues create economic dependency on the industry
- False "green" claims by AI companies regarding renewable energy while driving fossil fuel generation at the grid margin

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 3 (DRC miner — extractive industry harms), Stakeholder 5 (artist — skepticism of AI industry claims), Stakeholder 6 (AI safety researcher — critique of unchecked AI scaling)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company executive), Stakeholder 2 (chip supply chain), Stakeholder 7 (government regulator focused on economic growth)

---

### Stakeholder 5: Content Creator / Visual Artist or Writer

**Role/Title:** Independent Author, Illustrator, or Journalist Whose Work Was Scraped for AI Training

**Who they represent:** The millions of artists, novelists, journalists, photographers, and other creative workers whose copyrighted work was included in AI training datasets without consent or compensation. This includes plaintiffs in active cases (New York Times, Authors Guild, Getty Images, musician coalitions) as well as the broader creative class affected by AI-generated content competing with their livelihoods.

**Primary Interests:**
- Legal recognition that using copyrighted works for commercial AI training requires licensing or constitutes infringement
- Compensation — either retroactive licensing or ongoing royalty structures — for use of their intellectual property
- Transparency about what works were included in training datasets
- Preservation of viable markets for human-created content as AI-generated alternatives proliferate

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Unauthorized scraping of their specific works without attribution or payment
- AI companies claiming fair use as blanket protection for commercial training on copyrighted material
- Economic displacement: AI image generators trained on their style can produce unlimited competing images at near-zero marginal cost
- NDA agreements in any settlements that prevent public disclosure of training data practices

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice — shared critique of AI industry accountability), Stakeholder 6 (AI safety researcher — ethical development concerns)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company executive — defends fair use), Stakeholder 7 (regulator — may prioritize U.S. AI competitiveness over creator rights)

---

### Stakeholder 6: AI Safety and Ethics Researcher

**Role/Title:** Senior Research Scientist, AI Safety Organization (e.g., AI Now Institute, Distributed AI Research Institute [DAIR], or university AI ethics lab)

**Who they represent:** The academic and civil society community studying AI's systemic risks and ethical dimensions — including researchers focused on environmental impact, algorithmic harm, labor exploitation, and the governance of AI development. This stakeholder occupies a unique position: often having technical credibility within the AI industry but advocating for constraint and accountability.

**Primary Interests:**
- Rigorous documentation of AI's environmental and social costs across the supply chain
- Policy frameworks for mandatory disclosure (energy use, water use, training data provenance)
- International governance mechanisms to prevent a race to the bottom on AI safety and labor standards
- Supporting alternative AI development paradigms (smaller, more efficient models; federated approaches) that reduce resource extraction

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Systemic under-counting of AI's true lifecycle carbon and water footprint
- Lack of regulatory mechanisms to hold AI companies accountable for supply chain labor abuses
- The "efficiency paradox": efficiency gains (like DeepSeek) may lower per-query costs but increase total AI usage and aggregate resource consumption (Jevons paradox)
- Concentration of AI production capacity in a few companies that have weak accountability to affected communities

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice), Stakeholder 5 (content creator), Stakeholder 3 (DRC miner — systemic critique)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company — research often inconvenient), Stakeholder 2 (chip supply chain — prefers voluntary standards)

---

### Stakeholder 7: Government Regulator — Trade, Energy, or Environmental Agency

**Role/Title:** Senior Policy Official, U.S. Department of Energy / Bureau of Industry and Security / EU AI Office (or equivalent national regulator)

**Who they represent:** National and supranational government bodies with jurisdiction over: AI chip export controls (BIS), data center energy and water use (DOE, state utility commissions), conflict mineral supply chains (SEC Section 1502, EU CS3D), environmental permitting, and emerging AI governance frameworks. This stakeholder faces genuine internal tensions — supporting AI as a strategic national industry while also enforcing environmental, trade, and labor standards.

**Primary Interests:**
- Maintaining U.S. (or EU) competitive advantage in AI against China
- Ensuring grid reliability and energy security as data center demand surges
- Enforcing conflict mineral disclosure requirements and labor standards in government procurement
- Building public legitimacy for AI through responsible governance without stifling innovation

**Key Concerns (extraction/production stage):**
- Export control efficacy: multiple chip generations have been circumvented by Chinese labs
- Grid stability: data center growth is outpacing transmission infrastructure
- Supply chain vulnerability: over-dependence on Taiwan for fab and China for rare earths
- Political pressure from both the AI industry (lobbying for fewer restrictions) and communities (demanding moratoriums and stronger environmental standards)

**Likely Allies:** Stakeholder 1 (AI company — on competitiveness), Stakeholder 2 (TSMC/Nvidia — on CHIPS Act implementation)

**Likely Opponents:** Stakeholder 4 (environmental justice — demands stronger environmental rules), Stakeholder 3 (DRC miner — labor enforcement gaps)
